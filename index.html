Quick Sort & Divide and Conquer

Q: What is the divide and conquer technique?

A: It is a problem-solving strategy that involves three steps: 1) **Divide** the problem into smaller sub-problems, 2) **Conquer** the sub-problems by solving them recursively, and 3) **Combine** the solutions of the sub-problems to get the solution for the original problem.

Q: What is the pivot element in quick sort?

A: The pivot is an element from the array that is chosen to partition the other elements. In the partitioning step, all elements less than the pivot are moved before it, and all elements greater than the pivot are moved after it. The pivot is then in its final sorted position.

Q: What is the worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort?

A: The worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort is O(n²). This happens when the pivot choices lead to extremely unbalanced partitions, such as when sorting an already sorted array.

Q: Which sorting algorithms use the divide and conquer technique?

A: Besides Quick Sort, another prominent sorting algorithm that uses the divide and conquer technique is **Merge Sort**.

0/1 Knapsack Problem

Q: What is the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

A: It is an optimization problem where you are given a set of items, each with a weight and a value, and a knapsack with a maximum weight capacity. The goal is to select a subset of items to put into the knapsack to maximize the total value, without exceeding the weight limit.

Q: What is the brute force approach in solving knapsack problems?

A: The brute force approach involves generating all possible subsets of the items (2ⁿ subsets for n items), calculating the total weight and value for each subset, and then choosing the subset that has the maximum value while still being within the weight capacity.

Q: Why is it called the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

A: It's called the "0/1" problem because for each item, you have only two choices: either you take the item (1) or you don't take it (0). You cannot take a fraction of an item.

Q: What is the main drawback in the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

A: The main drawback, especially for the brute force solution, is its high computational complexity of O(2ⁿ). This makes it very inefficient and impractical for a large number of items.

Dijkstra's Algorithm

Q: What is a greedy algorithm with its application?

A: A greedy algorithm makes the locally optimal choice at each step, hoping this will lead to a globally optimal solution. A key application is **Dijkstra's algorithm** for finding the shortest path in a graph.

Q: Why is Dijkstra's algorithm considered a greedy algorithm?

A: It is considered greedy because at every step, it selects the unvisited vertex with the smallest distance from the source. This is a locally optimal choice, made with the goal of finding the overall shortest path.

Q: What is the time complexity of Dijkstra's algorithm?

A: The time complexity depends on the implementation. Using an adjacency matrix or a simple array, it is O(V²). With a more efficient data structure like a binary heap (priority queue), it is O(E + V log V), where V is the number of vertices and E is the number of edges.

Q: What is a major limitation of Dijkstra's algorithm?

A: Its major limitation is that it does not work correctly for graphs with negative weight edges, as the greedy assumption no longer holds true in such cases.

Huffman Encoding

Q: Where is Huffman coding used?

A: Huffman coding is widely used for lossless data compression in file formats like JPEG and PNG, as well as in compression standards such as GZIP and PKZIP.

Q: Why is Huffman coding a lossless compression algorithm?

A: It is "lossless" because no data is lost during compression. The original data can be perfectly reconstructed from the compressed data without any degradation or loss of information.

Q: How does Huffman coding exhibit the greedy choice property?

A: Its greedy choice is to always merge the two nodes (characters or sub-trees) that have the lowest frequencies. By repeatedly making this locally optimal choice, it builds a globally optimal prefix code tree for compression.

Q: What is the main advantage of Huffman coding?

A: Its main advantage is its efficiency in compressing data where some characters appear more frequently than others. By assigning shorter binary codes to frequent characters and longer codes to infrequent ones, it significantly reduces the overall file size compared to fixed-length encoding.
