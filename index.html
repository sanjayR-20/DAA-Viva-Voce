Module II: Brute Force

This section covers the Brute Force method, a straightforward approach to problem-solving based on the problem's statement and definitions.

Q: What is the Brute Force approach?

A: It's a straightforward strategy for solving problems, usually based directly on the problem statement and definitions. It's often called the "just do it" approach because it generates every possible solution and checks them against the problem's constraints.

Q: What are the strengths and weaknesses of Brute Force?

Strengths: It has wide applicability, is simple to implement, and provides reasonable solutions for some important problems like string matching and matrix multiplication.
Weaknesses: It rarely yields efficient algorithms and can be unacceptably slow for large instances. It's not considered as constructive as other design techniques.

Q: Explain the Brute Force approach to the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP).

A: For the Traveling Salesman Problem, the brute force method uses exhaustive search. It generates every possible tour (Hamiltonian circuit), calculates the cost (total distance) of each tour, and then selects the one with the minimum cost. The time complexity is factorial, O((n-1)!), making it infeasible for all but the smallest number of cities.

Q: How does Brute Force solve the 0/1 Knapsack Problem?

A: It generates every possible subset of the given items. For each subset, it calculates the total weight and total value. If the total weight is within the knapsack's capacity, it compares the total value with the best solution found so far. This approach has a time complexity of O(2ⁿ), as there are 2ⁿ possible subsets for n items.

Module II: Divide and Conquer

This section covers the Divide and Conquer strategy, a well-known algorithm design technique that leads to recursive algorithms.

Q: What are the three main steps of the Divide and Conquer strategy?

A: The three steps are:
1. Divide: Break the problem into two or more smaller, independent sub-problems of the same type.
2. Conquer: Solve the smaller sub-problems recursively. If a sub-problem is small enough, solve it directly.
3. Combine: Merge the solutions of the sub-problems to obtain the solution for the original, larger problem.

Q: How does Merge Sort work?

A: Merge Sort follows the Divide and Conquer paradigm. It divides the input array into two halves, recursively calls itself for the two halves to sort them, and then merges the two sorted halves to produce the final sorted array. Its time complexity is O(n log n) in all cases (best, average, and worst).

Q: Explain the role of the 'pivot' in Quick Sort.

A: In Quick Sort, a pivot element is chosen from the array. The array is then partitioned: all elements smaller than the pivot are moved to its left, and all elements larger are moved to its right. After partitioning, the pivot is in its final sorted position. The algorithm then recursively sorts the sub-arrays to the left and right of the pivot.

Q: What is the worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort and when does it occur?

A: The worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort is O(n²). This occurs when the pivot selection consistently results in highly unbalanced partitions. For example, if the array is already sorted and the first or last element is always chosen as the pivot, one partition will have n-1 elements while the other has zero.

Module III: Greedy Technique

This section covers the Greedy Technique, where solutions are constructed through a sequence of steps making locally optimal choices.

Q: What is a Greedy Algorithm?

A: A greedy algorithm builds up a solution piece by piece, always choosing the next piece that offers the most obvious and immediate benefit. It makes a locally optimal choice at each stage with the hope of finding a global optimum. The choices made are irrevocable, meaning they are not changed in subsequent steps.

Q: Why is Dijkstra's algorithm considered a greedy algorithm?

A: Dijkstra's algorithm is greedy because at each step, it greedily selects the unvisited vertex with the smallest known distance (lowest cost) from the source. It always chooses the path that appears best at the moment, without considering the future steps. This locally optimal choice helps in constructing the shortest path from the source to all other vertices.

Q: What is Huffman Coding used for?

A: Huffman Coding is a technique used for lossless data compression. It uses a greedy approach to assign variable-length binary codes to characters based on their frequencies. More frequent characters get shorter codes, and less frequent characters get longer codes, which reduces the overall size of the encoded data.

Q: How does Huffman Coding exhibit the greedy choice property?

A: The greedy choice in Huffman's algorithm is to, at each step, merge the two nodes (characters or sub-trees) with the lowest frequencies. By repeatedly combining the two least likely characters into a new node, it builds an optimal binary tree (the Huffman Tree) that results in the most efficient, prefix-free binary code for the given set of characters.
