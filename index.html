Quick Sort & Divide and Conquer

What is the divide and conquer technique?

Click to see the answer

A: A strategy involving three steps: 1) **Divide** the problem into smaller sub-problems, 2) **Conquer** them recursively, and 3) **Combine** their solutions to solve the original problem.

What is the pivot element in quick sort?

Click to see the answer

A: The pivot is an element used to partition the array. Elements smaller than the pivot are moved to its left, and larger elements to its right, placing the pivot in its final sorted position.

What is the worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort?

Click to see the answer

A: O(n²). This occurs with unbalanced partitions, for example, when sorting an already sorted array and picking the first element as the pivot.

Which sorting algorithms use the divide and conquer technique?

Click to see the answer

A: Besides Quick Sort, another prominent sorting algorithm that uses the divide and conquer technique is **Merge Sort**.

0/1 Knapsack Problem

What is the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

Click to see the answer

A: An optimization problem to select items with given weights and values to maximize total value in a knapsack without exceeding its weight capacity.

What is the brute force approach in solving knapsack problems?

Click to see the answer

A: Generating all 2ⁿ possible subsets of items and selecting the one with the maximum value that fits within the weight capacity.

Why is it called the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

Click to see the answer

A: Because for each item, you have two choices: either take it (1) or leave it (0). You cannot take a fraction of an item.

What is the main drawback in the 0/1 Knapsack problem?

Click to see the answer

A: The main drawback of the brute force solution is its high computational complexity of O(2ⁿ), making it impractical for many items.

Dijkstra's Algorithm

What is a greedy algorithm with its application?

Click to see the answer

A: An algorithm that makes the locally optimal choice at each step, hoping to find a global optimum. A key application is Dijkstra's algorithm.

Why is Dijkstra's algorithm considered a greedy algorithm?

Click to see the answer

A: It's greedy because at each step, it selects the unvisited vertex with the smallest known distance from the source, which is a locally optimal choice.

What is the time complexity of Dijkstra's algorithm?

Click to see the answer

A: O(V²) with a simple array, but improves to O(E + V log V) using a priority queue (binary heap).

What is a major limitation of Dijkstra's algorithm?

Click to see the answer

A: Its major limitation is that it does not work correctly for graphs with negative weight edges, as the greedy assumption no longer holds true in such cases.

Huffman Encoding

Where is Huffman coding used?

Click to see the answer

A: For lossless data compression in formats like JPEG, PNG, GZIP, and PKZIP.

Why is Huffman coding a lossless compression algorithm?

Click to see the answer

A: Because the original data can be perfectly reconstructed from the compressed data without any loss of information.

How does Huffman coding exhibit the greedy choice property?

Click to see the answer

A: Its greedy choice is to always merge the two nodes (characters or sub-trees) with the lowest frequencies, which builds an optimal compression tree.

What is the main advantage of Huffman coding?

Click to see the answer

A: Its main advantage is assigning shorter codes to frequent characters and longer codes to infrequent ones, significantly reducing file size.
